## Why You Need to Ask the Right Questions

Choosing a photographer for your Indian wedding is one of the most important decisions you will make during planning. It is also one of the most stressful, because unlike a venue or a caterer — which you can visit and taste-test — photography is a service you cannot fully evaluate until after the wedding is over. By then, it is too late to fix anything.

I have heard too many stories from couples who hired a photographer based on a beautiful Instagram feed, only to discover on the wedding day that the photographer had never shot an Indian ceremony before, did not know what a baraat was, and missed half the pheras because they were changing a lens. That is heartbreaking. And it is entirely preventable.


The questions below are the ones I wish every couple would ask before booking anyone — including me. In fact, I welcome these questions. A confident photographer will answer them openly. A photographer who gets defensive or vague should raise red flags. Moreover, these questions will help you compare photographers on substance, not just style.

## Questions About Experience and Cultural Knowledge

### 1. How many Indian weddings have you photographed?

This is the most important question on the list. An [Indian wedding](https://mhphoto.ca/photography/indian-weddings/) is fundamentally different from a Western wedding. The pace, the number of events, the lighting conditions, and the significance of specific moments all require experience that cannot be faked. You want a number — not a vague "a few" or "several." Look for at least 10 to 15 complete Indian weddings in their portfolio.

### 2. Can you walk me through the key moments of a Hindu / Sikh / Muslim ceremony?

This is your cultural knowledge test. If the photographer cannot name the jaimala, the mangal pheras, the kanyadaan, the vidaai (for a Hindu wedding), or the Anand Karaj and its four Laavan (for a Sikh wedding), they do not know your traditions well enough to capture them. A knowledgeable photographer will not just name these moments — they will tell you where they position themselves for each one and why.

What to Listen For

A great answer includes specific positioning details: "During the pheras, I shoot from a low angle at the 2 o'clock position relative to the fire, so I get the couple in motion with the fire and mandap framing the shot." Vague answers like "I just follow the couple" are red flags.

### 3. Can I see full galleries from at least three Indian weddings?

Highlight reels are curated. Anyone can pick 20 great shots from a 2,000-image gallery. What you need to see is the full gallery — every delivered image from a complete multi-day celebration. This shows you their consistency, their ability to handle different lighting conditions, and whether they capture the quieter, in-between moments that make an album complete. If a photographer is unwilling to share full galleries, that is a significant concern.

### 4. Have you worked at my specific venue before?

Venue familiarity is a genuine advantage. A photographer who has shot at your Gurdwara knows where the natural light falls, where the best angles are, and what restrictions the management imposes. Similarly, knowing a banquet hall's lighting setup, stage layout, and power outlet locations means faster setup and fewer surprises. That said, a great photographer can adapt to any venue — this question is about preparation, not a dealbreaker.


### 5. Do you have experience with multi-faith or multi-cultural ceremonies?

If your wedding blends traditions — for example, a Hindu ceremony and a Western reception, or a Sikh-Christian celebration — you need a photographer who has navigated that before. The transitions between ceremonies, outfit changes, and different religious protocols require both cultural sensitivity and logistical experience. Ask for examples of similar blended celebrations they have photographed.

## Questions About Coverage and Logistics

### 6. How many days of coverage do you include?

A standard Western wedding package covers one day. An Indian wedding typically spans 2 to 5 days. If the photographer only offers single-day packages, they are not set up for Indian weddings. Look for photographers who offer dedicated [multi-day packages](/multiday-packages/) that cover mehndi, sangeet, haldi, ceremony, and reception as a complete package.

### 7. Will you bring a second photographer?

For Indian weddings, a second photographer is not a luxury — it is a necessity. During the baraat, one photographer needs to be with the groom's procession while the other captures the bride's preparations. During the ceremony, one shooter is at the mandap while the other captures family reactions. During the reception, one covers the dance floor while the other captures candids. If a photographer tells you one shooter is enough for an Indian wedding, I would respectfully disagree.

An Indian wedding without a second photographer is like trying to film a movie with one camera. You will capture the main action, but you will miss half the story.

### 8. How do you handle the baraat?

The baraat is one of the most dynamic and chaotic events to photograph. There are hundreds of people dancing in the street, dhol players creating vibration and noise, and the groom is arriving on foot or horseback. Ask the photographer how they approach this: Do they lead from the front? Do they have a system for the second shooter? Have they shot baraats in winter (a real consideration in Edmonton)? The level of detail in their answer tells you how prepared they are.

### 9. What happens if you get sick or have an emergency?

This is a question every couple should ask, regardless of the wedding style. For an Indian wedding, it is even more critical because of the multi-day commitment. A professional photographer will have a network of qualified backup shooters and a written contingency plan in their contract. They should be able to name their backup by name and show you their work.

### 10. Can you coordinate with our videographer?

Indian weddings are almost always photographed and videographed simultaneously. The two teams need to coordinate positioning, especially during key moments like the pheras and the jaimala, where there is limited space around the mandap. Ask whether the photographer has experience working alongside video teams and how they avoid getting in each other's shots. ## Questions About Deliverables and Editing

### 11. How many edited photos will we receive?

For a multi-day Indian wedding, you should expect a minimum of 1,500 to 3,000 edited images — sometimes more for 4 to 5 day celebrations. Be wary of photographers who promise an unusually high number (5,000+) as this often means minimal editing, or an unusually low number (under 1,000) which suggests insufficient coverage. Ask whether every image is individually edited or batch-processed.

### 12. What is your editing style, and can it match the vibrant colours of Indian weddings?

This is crucial. Many Western wedding photographers favour a muted, desaturated editing style — soft pastels, faded blacks, low contrast. That style can drain the life out of an Indian wedding, where colour is everything. The deep reds, vibrant greens, rich golds, and bright oranges are central to the aesthetic. Your photographer's editing should enhance these colours, not mute them. Ask to see before-and-after editing examples from Indian weddings specifically.

### 13. When will we receive our final gallery?

Turnaround time varies significantly between photographers. For a multi-day Indian wedding, a reasonable timeline is 8 to 12 weeks for the full edited gallery, with sneak peeks delivered within 1 to 2 weeks. Be cautious of promises under 4 weeks for a large wedding — that often means rushed editing. On the other hand, anything over 16 weeks is unreasonably long.


## Questions About Business and Contracts

### 14. What is your payment structure and cancellation policy?

Standard practice is a retainer (25% to 50%) to secure the date, with the balance due 2 to 4 weeks before the wedding. The [Professional Photographers of Canada (PPOC)](https://ppoc.ca/) recommends always signing a detailed contract before any payments are made. Read the cancellation clause carefully — life happens, and dates sometimes change. A fair contract will outline clear terms for rescheduling (common with Indian weddings when auspicious dates shift) versus full cancellation. Ask about date-change policies specifically, as this is more relevant for Indian weddings than outright cancellation.

### 15. Do we own the rights to our photos?

This one matters more than most people realize. Some photographers retain copyright and charge extra for full-resolution images, printing rights, or album reprints. Others provide full personal-use licensing from the start. Ask specifically about: Can you print photos anywhere? Can you share on social media without watermarks? Can the photographer use your images in their portfolio? A transparent photographer will have clear answers to all of these.

Contract Red Flags

Watch out for contracts that: (1) do not include a specific delivery timeline, (2) charge extra for full-resolution files, (3) have no backup photographer clause, (4) do not specify how many edited images you will receive, or (5) include vague language about "creative direction" that gives the photographer too much control over your coverage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

Should I prioritize experience with my specific tradition (Hindu, Sikh, Muslim) over general Indian wedding experience?
Ideally, both. A photographer who has shot 50 Hindu weddings but never a Sikh Anand Karaj will still face a learning curve at a Gurdwara. That said, a photographer with broad Indian wedding experience can learn specific traditions quickly. Ask whether they have shot your specific ceremony type, and if not, whether they are willing to attend a pre-wedding consultation to learn the order of events.

Is it okay to ask for references from past Indian wedding clients?
Absolutely, and any professional photographer should be happy to provide them. Speaking with a past client gives you insight into the photographer's reliability, professionalism, and how they handle the unique pressures of an Indian wedding — things you cannot judge from a portfolio alone. Ask at least two references about their overall experience.

How far in advance should I book for an Indian wedding?
For peak wedding season (June through September), book 12 to 14 months in advance. Indian wedding dates often fall on auspicious days from the Hindu or Sikh calendar, and these dates can overlap with peak booking periods. Resources like [Maharani Weddings](https://www.maharaniweddings.com/) and [South Asian Bride](https://www.southasianbride.com/) recommend early booking as a top planning priority. Off-season weddings (October through March) offer more flexibility, but 8 to 10 months ahead is still recommended for top-tier photographers.

What should I budget for Indian wedding photography in Edmonton?
For multi-day coverage with a second photographer, professional editing, and a full gallery, expect to invest $5,000 to $12,000 CAD depending on the number of days and events.
