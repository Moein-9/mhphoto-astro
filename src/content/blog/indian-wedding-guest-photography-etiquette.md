## Why Photography Etiquette Matters at Indian Weddings

Indian weddings are some of the most visually stunning celebrations in the world. The colours, the outfits, the decorations, the rituals — everything is incredibly photogenic. So it makes complete sense that every guest wants to capture the beauty. However, there is a delicate balance between documenting the celebration and disrupting it.

As a photographer who has covered dozens of [Indian weddings in Edmonton](https://mhphoto.ca/photography/indian-weddings/) and across Alberta, I have seen it all. Guests stepping into the aisle during the pheras. Phones blocking the priest's view during the sindoor ceremony. Flash photography during intimate moments that are meant to be sacred and private. These things happen because most guests simply do not know the etiquette.

And that is perfectly understandable. If you have never attended an Indian wedding before, or if you have only been to a few, you might not know which moments are sacred and which are the perfect time to snap away. Moreover, even within Indian weddings, traditions vary enormously between Sikh, Hindu, Muslim, and South Indian ceremonies. Each has its own sacred moments and its own expectations.

## When It Is Okay to Take Photos

Let me be clear: Indian weddings have plenty of moments where guest photography is not just acceptable — it is encouraged. The key is knowing which moments those are.

### The Baraat

The baraat (groom's procession) is essentially a dance party. Everyone is singing, dancing, clapping, and celebrating the groom's arrival. This is absolutely the time to take photos and videos. In fact, the more the merrier. Your perspective from within the crowd adds to the documentation of the event. The energy is contagious, and the groom's family typically loves seeing photos from different angles.

### Reception Events

The reception, sangeet (music night), mehndi (henna party), and cocktail hour are all social events where photography is completely welcome. These are party atmospheres. Dance floor videos, table selfies, photos with the couple during greeting rounds — all perfectly fine. In fact, many couples later ask guests to share their reception photos because they capture candid moments the professional photographer might have missed.

### Decor and Food

Before the ceremony starts, take all the decor photos you want. The flowers, the mandap, the table settings, the food stations — these are all fair game. The couple spent months planning these details, and they appreciate guests noticing and photographing them. Similarly, the food at Indian weddings is spectacular. Go ahead and photograph that butter chicken station.


### Group Photos with Friends

When the couple is making their rounds during the reception, greeting tables, and taking photos with guests, absolutely get your photos with them. This is expected and welcome. Just be mindful of the line. If there are 200 guests waiting, keep your group photo quick and efficient. One or two shots, big smiles, and move along so everyone gets their turn.

Guest Tip

If you want a great photo with the couple, ask them during the reception when they are making their rounds. Do not pull them aside during the ceremony or during their formal portrait time with the photographer. That time is limited and planned in advance.

## When to Put Your Phone Away

This is the section that matters most. There are specific moments during an Indian wedding where your phone or camera should be in your pocket. Not in your hand, not held discreetly at your side — in your pocket.

### During the Pheras (Sacred Circles)

The pheras — the seven circles around the sacred fire — are the most important ritual in a Hindu wedding ceremony. This is the moment the couple becomes married. It is sacred, spiritual, and deeply meaningful. The professional photographer is positioned to capture every step, every expression, every blessing from the priest. When guests stand up with their phones or step into the aisle, they block the photographer's sightline. This can mean the difference between capturing a once-in-a-lifetime expression and missing it entirely.

### During the Anand Karaj (Sikh Ceremony)

In a Sikh wedding, the Anand Karaj takes place in the Gurdwara (temple). For more on the spiritual significance of the Anand Karaj, the [Sikh Heritage Foundation](https://www.sikhheritage.ca/) offers excellent educational resources. This is a deeply religious ceremony, and photography etiquette in a Gurdwara is strict. Many Gurdwaras have rules about photography — some allow only the official photographer, some prohibit flash, and some do not allow phones at all. Furthermore, you are expected to cover your head, remove your shoes, and sit respectfully. Your phone should not be a distraction during prayers.

### During the Sindoor and Mangalsutra

The sindoor (vermillion in the bride's hair parting) and mangalsutra (sacred necklace) moments are incredibly intimate. The groom is performing these rituals for his bride, and the emotions are raw and real. This is when you might see the bride's tears, the groom's shaking hands, the parents crying in the background. The professional photographer is capturing all of this from carefully planned positions. A flash from a guest's phone at this moment is not just distracting — it can ruin the photo.


### During Formal Portraits

After the ceremony, the couple typically has 30 to 60 minutes for formal portraits with their photographer. This might include couple portraits, family groupings, and bridal party shots. During this time, well-meaning guests often follow along, taking their own photos from behind the photographer. This is problematic for two reasons. First, it divides the couple's attention — they look at one camera, then another, resulting in neither photo being great. Second, the photographer has spent years learning how to direct light, poses, and expressions. A guest shooting from a different angle with flash can create competing light sources that affect the professional images.

The best gift you can give the couple is your full presence during their sacred moments. The professional photographer has it covered. Your job is to be a witness, not a documentarian.

Important Note

If the couple has announced an "unplugged ceremony," respect it fully. This means no phones, no cameras, no tablets during the ceremony. The couple has made this request because they want their guests to be present and they trust their professional photographer to capture everything. It is a loving decision, not a restrictive one.

## Practical Tips for Guest Photography

Alright, so you want to take some great photos without being "that guest." Here are my practical recommendations based on years of experience:

### Turn Off Your Flash

This is the number one rule. Your phone's flash is weak, produces harsh light, and it is distracting. It also fires at exactly the wrong moment — when the room is dimly lit for ambiance. The professional photographer is using carefully placed off-camera lighting that works with the venue's atmosphere. Your phone flash works against it. Moreover, in a dark ceremony space, a sudden flash from the audience is genuinely jarring for the couple, the priest, and everyone around you.

### Do Not Stand in the Aisle

During the ceremony, stay in your seat. The aisle is the photographer's workspace. If you step into the aisle with your phone, you are directly in the photographer's line of sight. I cannot tell you how many times I have had the perfect shot of the groom seeing his bride for the first time, only to have a guest step into the aisle at the exact wrong moment. It is heartbreaking for the couple when they see their gallery and realize a guest's phone is visible in their most important photo.

### Use Silent Mode

Your phone's shutter sound might seem quiet to you, but in a silent ceremony space with 300 people, it carries. Put your phone on silent. Furthermore, turn off all notifications. A text message alert during the exchange of garlands is not ideal.

### Be Aware of Your Position

If you are going to take photos during the reception or baraat, be aware of where the professional photographer and videographer are working. Do not position yourself directly in front of them. If you see a photographer moving toward you with their camera raised, step aside. They have a shot in mind, and they only have seconds to get it.

### Quality Over Quantity

You do not need to record the entire ceremony on video. Your phone video will never match the quality of the professional videographer's work. Instead, capture a few short clips of your favourite moments during appropriate times, and spend the rest of the celebration actually enjoying it. The couple will share their professional photos with you later, and those will be the ones you actually want to look at.


## Social Media Etiquette After the Wedding

You took some great photos at the reception. Now what? Before you post them everywhere, consider a few things.

### Ask the Couple First

Some couples want to share their own professional photos before anyone else posts. This is especially true for couples who have not yet announced their wedding on social media. A quick text — "Hey, is it okay if I post the photos from last night?" — takes five seconds and shows tremendous respect. In addition, some families prefer that wedding photos are not shared publicly due to cultural or personal reasons. Always ask.

### Avoid Posting Unflattering Photos

This seems obvious, but it happens all the time. A blurry photo of the bride eating, a video of someone dancing awkwardly, a photo that catches someone at an unflattering angle — these should stay in your camera roll, not on Instagram. The golden rule is simple: if you would not want this photo posted of you, do not post it of someone else.

### Give the Photographer Credit

If the couple shares their professional photos with you and you repost them, tag the photographer. It is a small gesture that means a lot to us. Professional wedding photography is a small business, and word-of-mouth referrals — including social media tags — are how we grow. If you loved the couple's photos, help others find the photographer who created them.

Social Media Tip

If the couple has created a wedding hashtag, use it for all your posts. This makes it easy for the couple to find and collect all the guest photos in one place. It also helps other guests discover each other's photos.

Key Takeaways

- Take photos freely during baraats, receptions, sangeets, mehndi, and social events
- Put your phone away during pheras, Anand Karaj, sindoor, kanyadaan, and vidaai
- Always turn off your flash — it ruins the atmosphere and distracts the couple
- Never stand in the aisle during the ceremony — that is the photographer's workspace
- Ask before posting on social media, especially if the couple has not posted yet
- Respect unplugged ceremony requests fully — the couple trusts their photographer
- Your phone video will never match professional quality, so be present instead

## Frequently Asked Questions

Is it rude to take photos at an Indian wedding?
Not at all — Indian weddings are celebratory and most families love seeing guests capture the joy. The key is timing. Social events like the baraat, reception, and sangeet are perfect for guest photos. Sacred ceremonies like the pheras and Anand Karaj are times to put your phone away and be present.

What does "unplugged ceremony" mean?
An unplugged ceremony means the couple has requested no phones, cameras, or tablets during the wedding ceremony. They want their guests to be fully present and trust that their professional photographer and videographer will capture everything. This is becoming increasingly common at weddings of all cultures.

Can I use flash at an Indian wedding reception?
During the reception party, flash is less of an issue since the atmosphere is more social. However, I still recommend keeping flash off on your phone. It rarely improves photo quality and can be distracting to other guests and the professional photographers who are managing their own lighting setups.

Should I share my wedding photos with the couple?
Most couples love receiving guest photos, especially candid shots from the reception and baraat. Create a shared album or use the wedding hashtag. Just avoid sharing unflattering images, and ask before posting publicly on social media.

What if I am attending my first Indian wedding?
Congratulations — you are in for an incredible experience. Indian weddings are warm, welcoming, and joyful. For helpful primers on what to expect, [Maharani Weddings](https://www.maharaniweddings.com/) publishes excellent guides for first-time guests. Dress colourfully (avoid white and black for Hindu ceremonies), be open to participating in rituals when invited, respect the sacred moments, and enjoy the food. The family will appreciate your enthusiasm and respect.
