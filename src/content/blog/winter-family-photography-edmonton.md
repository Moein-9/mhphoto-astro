## Why Winter Family Photos Are Worth the Cold

I know what you are thinking. It is minus twenty in Edmonton, snow is blowing sideways, and I am suggesting you take your family outside for photos. It sounds wild. But hear me out — winter family photography in Edmonton produces some of the most breathtaking portraits you will ever see. And I say that having photographed families in every season for years.

There is something about a snowy Edmonton landscape that transforms ordinary family portraits into something genuinely magical. The clean white backdrop eliminates visual clutter. The soft winter light wraps around faces without harsh shadows. And the cozy warmth of a family huddled together against the cold creates an intimacy that summer sessions rarely match.

Here is the honest truth. Most families avoid winter sessions because they assume it will be miserable. But when you plan it right — the right location, the right clothing, and the right photographer who knows how to work fast in the cold — a winter session takes about 30 to 45 minutes. That is it. And the results are absolutely stunning.

## Best Edmonton Locations for Snowy Portraits

Edmonton's winter landscape offers a surprising variety of portrait locations. The key is finding spots that combine beautiful snow coverage with wind protection and easy access to warm vehicles. Here are my favourites after years of shooting winter sessions across the city.

### River Valley Trails

The North Saskatchewan River Valley is Edmonton's crown jewel for winter photography. The snow-covered trails, the frosted trees, and the dramatic river views create a backdrop that looks like a winter fairyland. My favourite spots include the trails near Emily Murphy Park and the Terwillegar Park footbridge area. The tree canopy provides natural wind protection, and the parking lots are close enough that families can warm up in their vehicles between setups.

### Government House Park

The grounds around Government House offer a more manicured winter look. The snow-covered lawns, the elegant architecture in the background, and the beautiful iron fencing create a timeless, classic portrait setting. It is particularly beautiful right after a fresh snowfall when everything is pristine and untouched.

![Winter family portrait session in Edmonton snowy landscape with frosted trees](https://mhphoto.ca/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/1.jpg)*Edmonton winters create a natural white studio that no indoor backdrop can match*

### Hawrelak Park

Hawrelak Park is stunning in winter. The amphitheatre area with its curved pathways and the surrounding evergreen trees provides variety within a single location. You can get wide landscape shots, intimate tree-lined portraits, and playful action shots of kids running through the snow all within a five-minute walk.

### Your Own Neighbourhood

Do not overlook the beauty right outside your front door. A snow-covered neighbourhood street, your own backyard with string lights, or a nearby park — sometimes the most meaningful photos come from the most familiar places. There is something special about looking at a winter portrait and knowing exactly where it was taken.

Location Tip

Always have a backup warm-up location planned. A nearby coffee shop, your vehicle with the heater running, or a sheltered park structure. Knowing you can warm up quickly makes everyone more relaxed and willing to enjoy the session.

## What to Wear for Winter Family Photos

Wardrobe choices make or break a winter portrait session. The goal is to look intentionally coordinated — not matching, but harmonious — while staying genuinely warm. Here is my detailed guidance.

### Colour Palette

Winter's white and grey backdrop means your clothing colours really pop. The most photogenic palettes for snowy portraits include deep jewel tones like burgundy, navy, emerald, and plum. Neutral earth tones like camel, cream, and rust also photograph beautifully against snow. Avoid all-white outfits that blend into the background and neon colours that compete with the serene winter mood.

### Layers Are Your Best Friend

Layers serve a double purpose. They keep you warm, and they add visual texture and depth to your portraits. A chunky knit sweater under a wool coat. A plaid scarf over a solid jacket. Layering creates visual interest and gives the photos dimension that a single flat jacket cannot.

### Kids' Outfits

For children, warmth wins over style every time. A cold, uncomfortable child will not smile naturally no matter how cute their outfit is. Dress kids in their warmest snow gear and add a coordinating scarf or hat that ties into the family's colour palette. During the session, I work quickly with kids and capture their natural joy — throwing snow, catching snowflakes, running through drifts. Those authentic moments are always the favourites.

The best winter portraits happen when everyone is warm enough to forget about the cold and just enjoy being together.

## Timing and Light: When to Schedule

Edmonton's winter light is absolutely gorgeous — but the window is short. In December and January, the sun sits low on the horizon almost all day, creating the soft golden light that portrait photographers dream about. However, usable daylight hours are limited.

Here is my recommended timing for winter family sessions in Edmonton based on the month:

- **November:** 2:30 PM to 4:00 PM for golden hour warmth
- **December:** 2:00 PM to 3:30 PM (shortest days, earliest golden hour)
- **January:** 2:30 PM to 4:00 PM as days slowly lengthen
- **February:** 3:00 PM to 4:30 PM with noticeably longer light
- **March:** 4:00 PM to 5:30 PM as spring approaches

The low winter sun is actually a gift for photography. It creates long, soft shadows and a warm golden glow that summer's harsh overhead sun simply cannot produce. I have photographed families in July at 7:00 PM and in January at 3:00 PM, and the January light is often more beautiful.

## Keeping Everyone Warm and Happy

The single most important factor in a successful winter session is comfort. If people are shivering, their smiles look forced. If their hands are numb, their body language is tense. Here is how I ensure every winter session feels comfortable and fun.

### Before the Session

I tell every family to arrive already warm. Crank the car heater on the drive over. Wear hand warmers inside your gloves. Bring a thermos of hot chocolate. Starting warm is much easier than trying to warm up once you are already cold.

### During the Session

I work fast in winter. My sessions are structured to move efficiently from setup to setup without long pauses. I know my locations intimately, so there is no wandering around looking for the right spot. We go directly to the first location, shoot for 10 to 15 minutes, move to the next, and repeat. Built-in warm-up breaks every 15 minutes keep everyone comfortable.

### Hot Chocolate Break

I always recommend families bring a thermos of hot chocolate to their winter session. Not just for warmth — though that helps enormously — but because the act of sipping hot chocolate together creates beautiful, natural photo opportunities. A family huddled together, hands wrapped around warm mugs, genuine smiles from the simple pleasure of a warm drink. Those candid moments are gold.

Warmth Hack

Adhesive hand warmers are inexpensive and last for hours. Stick them inside gloves and inside coat pockets before the session. For kids, tuck one inside each boot. The warmth makes a remarkable difference in everyone's comfort level and natural expressions.

## Tips for Photographing Kids in Winter

Children and snow are a magical combination — when handled right. Kids either love the snow and want to play in it endlessly, or they are cold and miserable within minutes. There is rarely an in-between. Here is how I approach winter sessions with young children.

First, I let them play. Before any posed shots, I give kids five minutes to run, throw snowballs, make snow angels, and just be kids in the snow. This burns off initial energy, warms them up through activity, and produces some of the best candid shots of the entire session.

For toddlers, I keep things extremely quick. We do family group shots first while everyone is fresh and warm. Then a few individual shots of the toddler in a parent's arms or playing in the snow. The entire toddler-focused portion takes five to seven minutes. That is their limit in cold weather, and I respect it completely.

For older kids — ages five and up — I lean into interactive prompts. "Who can throw a snowball the highest?" "Run to that tree and back." "Whisper a secret to your mom." These prompts create genuine reactions and natural movement that always photographs better than "stand here and smile."

## Photography Gear in Extreme Cold

Edmonton winters test equipment like nowhere else. At minus 25 or minus 30, camera batteries drain in minutes, LCD screens slow down, and metal camera bodies become painful to touch with bare hands. Here is how I prepare my gear for every winter session.

- **Batteries:** I carry four to six fully charged batteries and keep the spares warm in an inside pocket against my body. Cold batteries die fast but recover when warmed up. Rotating between batteries ensures I never lose a shot.
- **Camera body:** I use weather-sealed professional bodies that handle extreme cold without issue. The rubber grips provide insulation against the cold metal.
- **Lenses:** Condensation is the enemy. When moving from cold outdoors to a warm vehicle, I wrap the camera in a sealed plastic bag before going inside. This lets condensation form on the bag, not on the lens and sensor.
- **Memory cards:** Cold does not affect memory cards, but my frozen fingers make it harder to swap them. I use high-capacity cards to minimize changes.

The most important piece of gear for a winter photographer is not camera-related at all. It is my own clothing. If I am shivering and uncomfortable, I cannot focus on creating beautiful images for your family. I dress in layers, wear insulated boots, and bring my own hand warmers. Your comfort matters, and so does mine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

How cold is too cold for outdoor family photos?
I generally draw the line at minus 25 Celsius without wind chill. Below that, it becomes unsafe for children and the camera equipment struggles significantly. Between minus 15 and minus 20 is actually the sweet spot — cold enough for beautiful frost and snow, warm enough to stay comfortable for 30 to 45 minutes.

What happens if it is snowing during our session?
Light to moderate snowfall is actually beautiful in photos. Snowflakes catching the light, landing on shoulders and eyelashes — it adds a magical quality that clear skies cannot match. Heavy blizzard conditions warrant a reschedule, but gentle snow is a gift.

Can we do some photos indoors and some outdoors?
Absolutely. Many families start with outdoor snowy portraits and then move inside to a warm coffee shop, their home, or a beautiful indoor location for additional shots. This combination gives you variety and keeps everyone comfortable.

My baby is under one year old. Can we still do a winter session?
Yes, with precautions. For babies under one, I keep the outdoor portion very short — five to ten minutes maximum. We do most posed shots with baby bundled in a parent's arms. Many families bring a portable blanket or fur throw for quick setups. The results are absolutely adorable.

When is the best month for winter photos in Edmonton?
Late November through early February offers the most consistent snow coverage and the most beautiful light. December is my favourite because the snow is fresh, the light is golden, and many families incorporate holiday elements into their session.
