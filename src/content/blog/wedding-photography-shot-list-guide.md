## Why Every Couple Needs a Shot List

Here is something I tell every couple I work with: your wedding day will move faster than you think. There will be moments you planned for months that pass in thirty seconds. There will be people you love who you barely get to speak to. And there will be details — your grandmother's borrowed earrings, the handwritten vows you spent weeks perfecting, the custom cocktail napkins your sister designed — that you will want to remember but might not even notice in the moment.

A shot list is your safety net. It is a simple document that ensures your [wedding photographer](https://mhphoto.ca/photography/weddings/) knows exactly which moments, people, and details matter most to you. It does not replace your photographer's creativity or instinct — a good photographer will capture thousands of beautiful candid moments without any direction at all. But it guarantees that the specific shots you care about most are never missed.

After photographing hundreds of weddings in Edmonton and beyond, I have developed a comprehensive shot list that covers every phase of the wedding day. This guide walks you through each category, explains why each shot matters, and gives you a free checklist you can customize and share with your photographer.

## Getting Ready Shots

The getting-ready phase sets the tone for the entire gallery. It is intimate, emotional, and often where the most genuine candid moments happen. Your photographer should arrive at least 90 minutes before the ceremony to capture this phase fully.

### The Bride's Preparation

- **Dress hanging in natural light:** Near a window, on a beautiful hanger (consider a custom hanger with your new last name)
- **Shoes, jewellery, and accessories laid out:** Flat lay or arranged on a clean surface
- **Hair and makeup in progress:** Candid shots of the process, not just the finished result
- **Mother helping with the dress:** One of the most emotional moments of the day — the zip, the buttons, the moment Mom sees her daughter as a bride
- **Bridesmaids' reaction:** The first look from the bridal party when the bride is fully dressed
- **The veil moment:** Putting on the veil, the dupatta, or the headpiece
- **Reading a letter from the partner:** If exchanging letters before the ceremony, this is a beautiful moment to capture
- **Father's first look:** Often the most tear-inducing moment of the entire getting-ready phase

### The Groom's Preparation

- **Suit or sherwani details:** Cufflinks, tie, turban, pagdi, boutonniere
- **Getting dressed:** Groomsmen helping with the turban wrap, adjusting the jacket, straightening the tie
- **Best man moment:** The best man and groom sharing a quiet word before the day begins
- **Family blessings:** In many cultures, the groom receives blessings from elders before leaving for the ceremony
- **Mirror shot:** The groom checking himself in the mirror — a quiet moment of anticipation


Photographer Tip

Choose a getting-ready room with large windows and minimal clutter. Natural light is essential for beautiful preparation photos. Clear the bed, countertops, and floor of personal items so the focus stays on you and your loved ones. Even a quick five-minute tidy makes a huge difference.

## Ceremony Must-Haves

The ceremony is the heart of the wedding. Every moment matters, and most of them happen only once. Here is the essential list.

### Pre-Ceremony

- **Venue wide shot:** The ceremony space fully set up before guests arrive
- **Detail shots:** Programs, flowers, aisle decor, mandap or altar details
- **Guests arriving:** Candid shots of family and friends finding their seats
- **The officiant:** Setting up, reviewing notes, greeting the couple's families

### During the Ceremony

- **The processional:** Every person walking down the aisle — bridesmaids, groomsmen, flower girl, ring bearer, parents, and of course the bride
- **First look at the altar:** The groom's face when the bride appears
- **The vows:** Close-ups of faces, hands, and the emotional reactions of both partners
- **Ring exchange:** Tight shot of the rings being placed on fingers
- **The kiss or ceremony conclusion:** The moment that seals the marriage
- **Guest reactions:** Parents crying, friends cheering, grandparents watching with pride
- **Cultural rituals:** Pheras around the fire, mangalsutra placement, circling the Guru Granth Sahib, breaking the glass — whatever traditions your ceremony includes
- **The recessional:** The couple walking back down the aisle as a married pair

For [Indian wedding ceremonies](https://mhphoto.ca/photography/indian-weddings/) specifically, the shot list expands significantly. The baraat procession, the milni, the jaimala, the kanyadaan, the saat pheras — each of these is a distinct ritual with its own emotional weight. An experienced photographer who understands the cultural significance of each ritual will know exactly when to position themselves for the best angles.

## Family Formals and Group Photos

Family formals are the photos your parents and grandparents will print and frame. They are also the most logistically challenging part of the day. Without a clear list and a plan, they can eat up 45 minutes or more. With a prepared list, you can get through all essential groupings in 15 to 20 minutes.

### The Essential Groupings

- Couple with bride's parents
- Couple with groom's parents
- Couple with both sets of parents
- Couple with bride's immediate family
- Couple with groom's immediate family
- Couple with bride's grandparents
- Couple with groom's grandparents
- Full bridal party (all bridesmaids and groomsmen)
- Bride with bridesmaids only
- Groom with groomsmen only
- Couple with all guests (wide group shot if venue allows)

Beyond these basics, every family is different. Maybe your aunt who raised you is like a second mother and deserves her own photo with the couple. Maybe your university roommates need a group shot. Maybe there are family members who have not all been in the same room in twenty years. These are the shots that should go on your personalized list.

Photographer Tip

Assign a family wrangler — a sibling, cousin, or wedding planner who knows everyone by name. This person stands near the photographer during formals and calls out the next grouping. It is the single most effective way to keep formals moving quickly and efficiently.

## Couple Portraits

Couple portraits are the photos that end up on your wall. They are the images you will look at decades from now and remember exactly how you felt on that day. I recommend blocking at least 30 minutes — ideally 45 — for just the two of you with your photographer.

### Classic Must-Haves

- **Walking together:** Hand in hand, looking at each other, laughing
- **Forehead touch:** Intimate, romantic, timeless
- **Full-length portrait:** Showing the complete outfit and venue backdrop
- **Close-up:** Faces together, rings visible, genuine expressions
- **Looking at each other:** Natural eye contact with genuine smiles
- **The dip or lift:** If comfortable, a dramatic pose that adds variety
- **Walking away:** Shot from behind, holding hands, heading into the next chapter

The best couple portraits happen when you forget the camera is there. I always spend the first five minutes just talking with the couple, letting them settle into each other's presence. Once the nervous energy fades and they start genuinely connecting — that is when the real photos begin.

A shot list tells your photographer what you want to remember. But the best photos are always the ones you did not plan for.

## Reception Coverage

The reception is a long event with many moving parts. Here is what should be on your shot list for complete coverage.

### Reception Essentials

- **Venue setup:** Wide shots of the fully decorated reception space before guests enter
- **Table settings:** Centrepieces, place cards, menu cards, favours
- **Grand entrance:** The couple's arrival into the reception
- **First dance:** Multiple angles — wide establishing shots and intimate close-ups
- **Parent dances:** Father-daughter, mother-son, or cultural equivalents
- **Speeches and toasts:** Both the speakers and the couple's reactions
- **Cake cutting:** The moment of cutting and feeding each other
- **Bouquet and garter toss:** If included in your reception
- **Dance floor:** Guests celebrating, Bollywood performances, choreographed dances
- **Send-off:** Sparklers, petals, confetti — the final exit as a married couple


## Detail and Decor Shots

Details are easy to overlook but incredibly meaningful. You spent months choosing each element of your wedding design. Your photographer should document all of it.

- **Invitation suite:** Flat lay with rings, florals, and accessories
- **Flowers:** Bouquet, boutonniere, ceremony arrangements, reception centrepieces
- **Rings:** On a textured surface, in a ring box, on the invitation, on a flower
- **Shoes:** Both bride's and groom's, styled or candid
- **Signage:** Welcome signs, seating charts, custom hashtags
- **Food and cake:** The buffet spread, cocktail hour appetizers, the wedding cake from multiple angles
- **Gift table:** Cards, gifts, any meaningful displays
- **Cultural elements:** Mandap details, mehndi designs, kalira, chooda, the Guru Granth Sahib setup

Photographer Tip

Gather all your small details — rings, jewellery, shoes, invitation, perfume — in one place before the photographer arrives. A dedicated detail tray or surface saves time and ensures nothing is forgotten. If you have a family heirloom or sentimental item you want photographed, place it with the other details and tell your photographer the story behind it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a wedding shot list be?
A good shot list typically includes 20 to 40 specific items across all phases of the day. It should be detailed enough to cover your priorities without being so long that it becomes a burden. Your photographer will naturally capture hundreds of additional moments beyond the list.

Should I give my photographer a Pinterest board of poses?
A few inspiration images are helpful for communicating your style preferences. Sites like [Junebug Weddings](https://junebugweddings.com/) and [The Knot](https://www.theknot.com/) have excellent galleries for inspiration. However, avoid sharing hundreds of pins — it can feel restrictive. Instead, share 5 to 10 images that represent the mood and feel you love, and trust your photographer to interpret them in a way that is authentic to you.

How much time should we budget for family formals?
With a prepared list and a family wrangler, you can complete essential family formals in 15 to 20 minutes. Without a list, the same photos can take 45 minutes or more as you try to remember who needs to be in which grouping. Preparation saves time and reduces stress.

What if we forget something on the shot list?
An experienced photographer will naturally capture the major moments even without a list. The shot list is specifically for personalized requests — that specific family grouping, that heirloom detail, that particular tradition. If you forget something, your photographer's instinct will likely cover it anyway.

Do Indian weddings need a longer shot list?
Yes. Multi-day Indian weddings with events like mehndi, sangeet, haldi, baraat, ceremony, and reception each need their own shot list. I work with my [multi-day package](/multiday-packages/) couples to build a comprehensive guide covering every event and every cultural ritual.
